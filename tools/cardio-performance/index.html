Created for running, the Riegel model can estimate performances in other endurance sports. The model can estimate performances in running, swimming, cycling, speed skating. The best documented sports, running, walking, and swimming are best predicted by the model. The Riegel model is accurate for the majority of endurance running events, ranging from 3.5 minutes to 230 minutes.

The exponent in the formula (called the "fatigue factor") determines the rate at which a competitor loses speed over distance. Different demographic's fatigue factors range from 1.03 in swimmers to 1.08 in world class runners.

The Reigel equation shows what price competitors must pay for better performance. According to Athletic Records and Human Performance, doubling specific endurance results in only a 5% increase in speed. Riegel made no claims that his model embodied a theory of human movement, but was a derivation from top athletic performances. The Riegel model is the most common use model and is the simplest to remember
