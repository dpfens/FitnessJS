Body composition has a few metrics and many ways of measuring them. These techniques range from measuring body surface area to measuring lean body mass. A more cost-effective way method is to use equations to estimate these values.

BMI and Corpulence measure are two normalized ratios for comparing composition in populations. These measures have different normalization techniques and have strengths and weaknesses. BMI is most most common formula, and attempts to quantify the amount of body tissue. BMI calculation is used to categorize participants as underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese. The accepted BMI ranges can differ depending on ancestry. BMI has significant and pervasive error margins, reducing its usefulness in health evaluation. Corpulence measure yields valid results for short and tall persons, but is sensitive to small changes in input values. Corpulence has some support as a more accurate measure than BMI.

Body fat percentage (%BF) is the measure of mass of body fat to total body mass. There are two types of body fat: essential fat, and storage fat. Essential body fat is necessary to maintain life and reproductive functions. According to the NASM, healthy men should have 2-5% body fat, and women should have 10-13%. Body fat percentage is a measure of relative composition. Underwater weighing and ADP are common methods for measuring body fat percentage.

Body surface area (BSA) is a measure of surface area of the body. BSA is a better indicator of metabolic mass than weight because it is less affected by adipose mass. BSA is common in clinic settings for estimating cardiac output and drug dosing.
